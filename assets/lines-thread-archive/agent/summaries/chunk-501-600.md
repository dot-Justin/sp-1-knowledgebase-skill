## Narrative

The Track1+4 bootloader discovery from the previous chunk pays off. After further analysis of the CDC serial protocol that activates during the 60-second window, the team reverse-engineers the bootloader command format well enough to send firmware updates.

**solderless.engineering launches** - a web-based firmware updater that works entirely through the browser. The user holds Track 1 + Track 4, plugs in USB-C, and the website handles flashing. No soldering, no opening the device, no J-Link required. The bootloader has been cracked.

This is a major accessibility milestone. The project goes from "you need a glitch rig or hardware expertise" to "anyone can update their device from a browser."

**SP-1-dev GitHub published:** TimK publishes [timknapen/SP-1-dev](https://github.com/timknapen/SP-1-dev) - the custom firmware repository. Code, schematic, documentation, and the audio format spec begin accumulating here.

**Community poll:** A poll is posted asking the community about interest in custom stem support. **120 responses; 97% want custom stems.** This validates the demand and motivates continued development.

**Timing metadata decode begins:** The eMMC audio sectors contain a field that doesn't obviously decode as audio. JoseJX and others begin examining it more carefully. Early hypothesis: it's a **16-bit counter** embedded in each chunk that encodes tempo/position information. Values appear to range from 0 to approximately 24,489.

The community begins cross-referencing this with MIDI clock concepts - the range and distribution of values hints at a musical timing encoding rather than raw sample offsets.
