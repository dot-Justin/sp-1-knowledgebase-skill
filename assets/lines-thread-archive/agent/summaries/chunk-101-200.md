## Narrative

This chunk covers the long grind of the glitch campaign - the central technical challenge of the project.

**murray** completes and publishes the automated BeagleBone Black PRU glitch tool: [resinbeard/beaglebone-black-glitcher](https://github.com/resinbeard/beaglebone-black-glitcher). The PRU (Programmable Realtime Unit) runs at a deterministic clock independent of the Linux OS, allowing nanosecond-precision voltage glitch timing. The rig connects to the nRF52840's VDD rail, pulses it low at the precise window after reset where APPROTECT is read from flash but before it is enforced.

Attempts are logged publicly. The counter climbs: hundreds, then thousands. The community watches the GitHub attempt log.

**elew** appears - an audio professional who owns **five units** and is willing to run parallel glitch attempts. This significantly increases throughput. elew's involvement also adds a second perspective on the device's origins and use in professional audio contexts.

Meanwhile, **JoseJX** begins analyzing the eMMC content available without firmware (the data partition is not protected). He reverse-engineers the audio storage format:
- **8,192-byte sectors**
- **2,048-byte chunks**
- **24-bit PCM audio at 48kHz**
- **8 interleaved channels** (4 stereo stems: drums, bass, melody, vocals)

The chunk data produces audible audio when decoded correctly - the Ye tracks are confirmed still intact on the eMMC.

The eMMC is also confirmed **writable** through standard eMMC command access - the protection is only on the nRF52840, not the storage. This will matter later.

Toward the end of this chunk (post ~200 range, January 25, 2025), **murray dumps the firmware**. On attempt **#8,504**, the APPROTECT glitch succeeds. A complete nRF52840 firmware image is extracted via J-Link SWD. The thread erupts.
